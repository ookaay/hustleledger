TL;DR: The Short

Record lectures with Otter.ai, upload to NotebookLM, generate study guides and practice questions, write your essay using Claude, and finalize with Grammarly. Total active time per lecture: 20 minutes.

I spent my first two years of university taking notes the traditional way — laptop open, frantically typing everything the professor said, missing half the discussion because I was looking at my keyboard. My grades were average, my notes were unusable by exam week, and I was chronically behind on readings.

Last fall, I rebuilt my entire note-taking workflow around AI tools. The result: my GPA went from 3.1 to 3.8, I stopped pulling all-nighters before exams, and for the first time, I actually had time for a social life.

Here's the exact system, step by step.

Step 1: Capture (During Lecture)

Open Otter.ai on your phone, tap record, and put your phone face-down on the desk. That's it. Don't touch it again until class ends.

Otter transcribes everything in real time. It identifies different speakers (professor vs. student questions). It generates a rough summary automatically.

Cost: Free (300 minutes per month, which covers about 10 lecture hours).

The key insight: do not take notes during lecture. Just listen. The transcription handles capture. Your job is comprehension.

format_list_numbered

Step 2: Process (10 minutes, after lecture)

Export the Otter transcript as text. Create a new Google NotebookLM notebook for this lecture. Upload:

  1. The Otter transcript
  2. The professor's slides (exported as PDF)
  3. Any assigned readings for this week

NotebookLM processes everything and lets you ask questions based only on your sources. Start with these three prompts:

  • "Summarize this lecture in 5 bullet points."
  • "Generate 10 practice questions from this material with answers."
  • "What concepts from this lecture connect to last week's material?"

Copy the Q&A into an Anki deck for spaced repetition review later.

Cost: NotebookLM is free. Anki is free.

format_list_numbered

Step 3: Review (5 minutes, before next class)

Open your Anki deck. Review the cards from last lecture. Anki shows you cards you're struggling with more often and cards you know less often. Total time: 5 minutes.

This is the step most students skip, and it's the most important one. Research shows that reviewing material within 24 hours of learning it improves retention by 50% or more. Anki automates this so you don't have to think about scheduling.

format_list_numbered

Step 4: Write (For essays and assignments)

When you have a paper due, open your NotebookLM notebooks for the relevant lectures. Ask NotebookLM to:

  • "Extract the three strongest arguments from my course material on [topic]."
  • "Find counterarguments and limitations mentioned in the sources."

Then open Claude (free tier). Paste the NotebookLM output and ask Claude to help structure your argument. Claude excels at long-form reasoning and essay structure.

Write your draft in Claude's interface, then copy to Google Docs.

format_list_numbered

Step 5: Polish (Before submission)

Run your draft through Grammarly (free). It catches:

  • Grammar and spelling errors
  • Unclear sentences
  • Tone inconsistencies (academic writing should stay formal)
  • Overused words

One pass through Grammarly typically improves a B paper to a B+ or A- just by cleaning up the writing.

The Results After One Semester

  • Average essay grade: 87% (was 74%)
  • Time spent on notes per week: 1.5 hours (was 6 hours)
  • Exam prep time: 3 hours per exam (was 2 days of cramming)
  • GPA: 3.8 (was 3.1)

Why This Works

The traditional note-taking model assumes you have to choose between understanding (listening in class) and capture (writing things down). AI tools break that trade-off. You capture everything automatically and process it later with AI assistance.

The pipeline doesn't skip the learning — it removes the mechanical overhead so you can focus on actual understanding.

Try It For Two Weeks

Commit to this pipeline for two weeks. Download Otter, set up NotebookLM, install Anki. After two weeks, compare your comprehension and time spent against your current method. Most students who try this never go back.
